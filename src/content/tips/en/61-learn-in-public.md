Shawn Wang (swyx) coined the term **"Learning in Public"** and turned it into a professional development philosophy: **share what you learn while you're learning it, not when you're an "expert"**.

## Traditional method vs. Learning in Public

```markdown
❌ Traditional method:
1. Learn in silence for years
2. Accumulate knowledge
3. Eventually, maybe, share something
4. Constant fear of impostor syndrome

✅ Learning in Public:
1. Learn something new
2. Share it immediately (blog, tweet, video)
3. Receive feedback and corrections
4. Improve your understanding
5. Repeat
```

## Why it works

### 1. The Feynman effect

```markdown
"If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough"

When writing about something, you discover gaps in your knowledge.
The act of explaining consolidates your learning.
```

### 2. Your personal documentation

```markdown
Problem 6 months ago: "How do I configure ESLint with TypeScript?"
- Google search: 2 hours
- Trial and error: 3 hours
- Frustration: incalculable

Problem today (if you wrote a post):
- Search your own blog: 30 seconds
- Copy the solution YOU documented
```

### 3. Involuntary networking

When you share, you attract people with similar interests:

- Recruiters looking for people who communicate well
- Other learners who want to collaborate
- Experts offering corrections (for free!)

## Formats to get started

### Blog posts

```markdown
# What I learned configuring Docker today

I came from knowing nothing about containers.
These are the mistakes I made and how I solved them.

## Error 1: Port already in use
[problem description and solution]

## Error 2: Container dies immediately
[problem description and solution]
```

### Tweets/threads

```markdown
🧵 Today I learned 5 things about CSS Grid I didn't know:

1/ grid-template-areas lets you "draw" your layout with ASCII art

2/ minmax() is magic for responsive without media queries

[continues...]
```

## Common fears

### "I'm not an expert to talk about this"

That's precisely why you should do it. Experts forget what was hard for beginners. Your novice perspective is valuable.

### "There are already 1000 articles about this"

But none written from YOUR perspective, with YOUR mistakes, in YOUR context.

### "What if I say something wrong?"

The community will correct you (gently, mostly). You learn faster with public feedback than in silence.

## Final reflection

Swyx transformed his career in 2 years applying this principle. Not because he was smarter, but because his learning was visible. In a world where everyone consumes content, being a creator automatically differentiates you.
